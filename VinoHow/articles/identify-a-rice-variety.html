How to Identify a Rice Variety

Rice is a popular food staple and various types grow all over the world with a myriad of different textures and colors.

By Juan Nepomuceno | jnpmcn@gmail.com

If you’re visiting a restaurant or trying to plan meals for your own household, you may have some trouble figuring out what type of rice you’re about to eat or what rice would work best for a recipe. Thankfully, it’s pretty easy to identify different types of rice by giving them a careful look.

STEP 1

Recognize glutinous rice as a really sticky variety of white rice.

Keep in mind that glutinous rice grains are both small and absorbent, and they have a tendency to stick to other grains of rice. You’ll likely find this type of rice in really thick recipes like porridge or rice balls. This rice doesn’t have a super distinct aroma, and it works well in a lot of different dishes.

  • Glutinous rice is a popular option in a lot of dessert recipes.

STEP 2

Pinpoint sushi rice by its oval grains and slight stickiness when cooked.

Note that sushi rice is fairly opaque when it’s raw, and gets a little sticky after it’s been cooked. Unlike glutinous rice, sushi rice doesn’t have a thick, porridge-like consistency. As the name suggests, you’ll likely find sushi rice as the base for a lot of sushi dishes.

  • Sushi rice has a good structure without losing its shape.

  • This rice isn’t known for having a special aroma.

STEP 3

Classify purple grains of rice as black rice.

Also known as “forbidden rice,” you can find black rice in specialty marketplaces and certain side dishes, like pilaf. The grains are straight instead of oval-shaped and become firm but slightly soft when cooked. Note that this rice is black when it's raw, and purple after it’s cooked.

  • Black rice is a popular option for some side dishes. If you notice any purple grains of rice in your pilaf, it’s likely black rice.

  • This type of rice isn’t known for having a distinct aroma.

STEP 4

Spot sweet brown rice by its small, translucent grains.

Note that sweet brown rice is a similar color to regular brown rice, but is a lot smaller and rounder. These grains will stick together once they’re cooked, unlike longer grains of brown rice.

  • This rice tends to lose a distinct texture after it’s cooked.
